# PROJECT CONCEPT

## Project Identity

This project is a world specification construction project.

Its primary purpose is to construct the specification of an independent fictional world that can serve as the stage for many different creative works.

The world specification itself is the main product of this project.

Games, novels, scenarios, tabletop settings, visual works, and other creative activities may later use this world, but those works are derivative uses. They must not be confused with the core project.

This is not a game development project with a worldbuilding section. It is not a novel project with background lore. It is a project whose central task is the construction of the world specification itself.

The HTML files produced in this project are not merely presentation pages or lore articles. They are working specification documents for the fictional world. Creative works that use this world should follow those specifications unless a specification is deliberately revised.

## Intended Use

The constructed world should be reusable as a shared stage for multiple works.

It should be possible to create different stories, games, campaigns, regions, historical periods, and characters within the same world without the world existing only to serve one specific plot or one specific game system.

The intended image is a setting-world that exists independently from the works that use it. Individual stories may occur within the world, but the world should not be reduced to the needs of those stories.

## Genre

The world is a sword-and-sorcery fantasy world.

It may contain magic, dragons, nonhuman species, supernatural phenomena, ancient ruins, gods, spirits, monsters, and other fantasy elements.

However, fantasy elements are not treated as permission for arbitrary or careless setting construction.

The world should be built with as much scientific, physical, historical, geographical, biological, social, and economic plausibility as possible.

## Scientific and Academic Plausibility

Real-world knowledge should be used wherever it can reasonably apply.

Geology, climate, ecology, biology, physics, chemistry, linguistics, anthropology, economics, military history, political history, and other fields may all be relevant when constructing the world.

Perfect scientific accuracy is not required, and some elements will inevitably contradict real-world science. However, departures from real-world science should be intentional, limited, and treated as part of the world's rules rather than as convenient exceptions.

If a dragon flies, its flight requires an explanation in relation to mass, lift, muscle power, skeletal structure, metabolism, atmosphere, or some defined supernatural mechanism.

If magic produces fire, the phenomenon should still be considered in relation to heat, fuel, oxygen, combustion, energy transfer, range, control, and consequences.

## Supernatural Premises

Some parts of the world cannot be fully explained by real-world science. Magic is the clearest example.

For such elements, the project may define supernatural premises: facts that are true in this world even if they do not exist in the real world.

These premises should not be used casually. They should be as few, as clear, and as structurally important as possible.

Once a supernatural premise is defined, it becomes part of the world's rules and must be respected in later setting work.

The goal is not to eliminate the supernatural, but to make the supernatural behave consistently enough that it can support believable world specification work.

## Internal Consistency

Internal consistency is one of the most important principles of this project.

Settings must not be added merely because they are attractive, familiar, convenient, or common in fantasy.

When a setting element is proposed, the first question is why it exists and why it works.

"A spell is cast, therefore a magical phenomenon occurs" is not sufficient by itself. The setting must address why casting a spell causes that phenomenon, what conditions allow it, what limits it, what it consumes, what can prevent it, and what consequences follow from it.

The answer to "why?" may be scientific, historical, geographical, social, economic, biological, or supernatural. If it is supernatural, that answer still becomes a rule of the world.

After a rule is established, later settings must obey it unless the rule itself is deliberately revised.

The process by which a setting becomes valid is important. A finished setting entry is less valuable if the reasoning that supports it is weak or absent.

## Relationship Between Setting and Explanation

This project rejects the approach of prioritizing the accumulation of setting facts while neglecting the reasons those facts exist.

A country, culture, species, religion, spell, monster, technology, trade route, language, or historical event should be treated as the result of causes and constraints.

The project should preserve not only final decisions, but also the reasoning that led to those decisions when that reasoning is important for future consistency.

## Relationship To Section-Specific Documents

Documents such as `WORLD_FOUNDATION.md` may contain important inherited information from previous work.

Such documents should be respected as section-specific guidance or established setting material, but they are not automatically the concept of the entire project.

For example, `WORLD_FOUNDATION.md` currently concerns geography, geology, human migration, historical development, and the causal relationship between physical conditions and civilization. It should be treated as an important foundation for that area of the world specification, not as the complete definition of the whole project.

This `PROJECT_CONCEPT.md` file defines the higher-level project concept.

## Working Principle

When developing this world, always distinguish between:

- the world itself
- works that may use the world
- section-specific setting documents
- project-wide principles

The world should remain broad, coherent, and reusable.

Creative works may adapt or emphasize parts of the world, but the construction of the world should not be subordinated to one derivative use unless that decision is made explicitly.
