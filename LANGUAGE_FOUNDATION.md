# LANGUAGE FOUNDATION

## Document Role

This document records working notes for constructed languages in the world specification project.

It is not a dictionary. Its purpose is to preserve the reasoning, historical assumptions, sound values, root meanings, and derivational potential behind language construction.

The first target is the language family used around the main peninsula. At this stage, the focus is the oldest layer of the proto-language, before the development of writing.

## Notation Policy

Words from periods before writing must not be treated as spellings.

For each early word or root, record:

- phonetic transcription: a project-use romanization for recording the sound
- Japanese reading: a practical reading aid
- IPA: a broad phonemic notation
- period: the historical language layer
- original function: the communicative situation in which the utterance arose
- early meaning: the range of meanings before later abstraction
- later derivational potential: likely meanings or grammatical functions that may develop later
- notes: reasoning and cautions

The romanized form is a working transcription, not an in-world orthography.

## Historical Context

The oldest roots currently under consideration belong to the proto-language of plainfolk groups around the main peninsula.

The relevant period is roughly 50,000 to 30,000 years before the present, after the route to the southwest continent was cut off and the northwestern continent side populations began developing a more independent linguistic system.

At this stage, words should be treated as early roots born in concrete life situations rather than modern dictionary words.

For example, the earliest water-related word `wau` is not a fully modern material noun, but it is also not merely a word for a place or situation. It refers to the broad undivided concept "water as a thing": the drinkable thing, water as a shared idea, remembered water, expected water, and water associated with rivers, ponds, shores, or the sea before those meanings have been separated.

During this same broad period, plainfolk groups shared parts of their living range with resident elf groups. The proto-main-peninsula language should therefore not be treated as developing in isolation.

Elf language was likely established earlier than the plainfolk language in the main peninsula region. Plainfolk groups in the peninsula had frequent contact with elf groups, so many early plainfolk roots should be treated as sharing a substrate with elf language, being shaped by elf usage, or being borrowed from elf forms.

The elf language of this period still had a different origin mechanism from the practical side of plainfolk language. Plainfolk roots tend to arise from movement, scouting, danger sharing, and practical group coordination. Early elf language should be treated as arising more from settled life, shared perception, emotional and sensory transmission, memory, taboo, tradition, and contact with spirits.

This does not mean early elf language began with advanced abstract philosophy. Its earliest distinctions should be concrete and experiential.

One important early elf distinction is between a broad concept-name and a terrain-form or topographic site in which that concept is recognized in the landscape.

The proposed old elf ending `-ia` / イァ / is now treated as a suffix for terrain-related forms.

Important: `-ia` is not currently treated as an independent root `ia` that later became a suffix. Instead, ordinary nouns first existed without a dedicated terrain-marking form. Later, speakers could add `-ia` when they wanted to mean a landform, terrain feature, geographic site, or old topographic name associated with that noun.

This is not a general article-like system and not a universal "concrete object" marker. It is a terrain-forming suffix used mainly for broad geographic features and old landform names.

Therefore, `-ia` should be understood as a terrain suffix: it turns a broad concept into a named or recognized terrain feature, geographic formation, or topographic site.

When this idea is carried into the plainfolk language through contact, it can attach to plainfolk roots as a borrowed or contact-induced category.

The key example is `wau-ia`.

The older plainfolk `wau` means "water as a thing" in a broad, undivided sense. Under elf influence, `wau-ia` develops as a form for a water-related terrain feature: sea, river-form, shore, waterside, or other recognized water landform.

Therefore:

- `wau` = water as a broad concept, before water as concept, place, source, surface, and substance are clearly separated.
- `wau-ia` = water-related terrain / geographic formation defined by water.

This should not be treated as a mandatory native plainfolk article. It is a contact form produced by bringing an elf terrain-forming pattern into plainfolk vocabulary.

In the earliest plainfolk-style report, discovering water is expressed as `ra wau`: "water found" or "there is water." As the plainfolk language mixes more deeply with elf language, `wau-ia` by itself becomes enough to report a recognized water terrain: "waterside", "river-form", "shore", or another established water-feature.

Because `wau-ia` can carry the result-report function by itself, `ra` becomes less necessary in that situation. The older positive-report use of `ra` retreats, and `ra` is increasingly used for acknowledgement, acceptance, agreement, affirmation, approval, and favorable result.

This retreat applies to active reporting, not to answers. If someone asks "is there water?" or gives an action that requires confirmation, `ra` remains appropriate as the passive affirmative response: "yes", "the asked thing is affirmed", or "there is." In contrast, when the speaker actively reports the presence of water without first being asked, `wau-ia` means "this is a water landform here" or "the water-feature in question is here."

## Oldest Root Set: Exploration Reports

### `wau`

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Phonetic transcription | `wau` |
| Japanese reading | ワゥ |
| IPA | /wau/ |
| Period | Oldest layer, proto-main-peninsula language |
| Original function | A loud call used to tell companions that water had been found or that water was relevant |
| Early meaning | Water as an undivided concept: the drinkable thing, expected water, remembered water, visible water, water associated with rivers, ponds, shores, or the sea |
| Weak form | `wa` / ワ / /wa/ |
| Later derivational potential | water, moisture, drinking, river, spring, pond, wetland, waterside, sea, water source |

`wau` is one of the oldest and most important roots.

Its final /u/ is weak. Even if the final sound is omitted, `wa` still communicates the water-related meaning. In actual use, a person who found water while moving or scouting could call `wau!` or `wa!` toward companions.

The root originally referred to "water as a thing" before later distinctions such as water as a material substance, a place where water is found, a body of water, remembered water, or water being discussed were clearly separated.

Later, under influence from an early elf terrain pattern, `wau-ia` can be used for a terrain feature defined by water. This distinguishes the older broad concept `wau` from a geographic formation such as shore, river-form, watercourse zone, or other named water-related terrain.

Because this call was extremely important for survival and had to be shouted over distance, other words from the same period should rarely begin with `wa-`. The `wa-` onset is effectively protected for water-related roots in the oldest layer.

Avoid later developments that make the water word resemble English "water", especially `wata`, `watar`, `water`, or similar forms.

### `am`

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Phonetic transcription | `am`; long-call form `aam` |
| Japanese reading | アァ（ム） / アーム |
| IPA | /aːm/; weak-final form /aː/ |
| Period | Oldest layer, proto-main-peninsula language |
| Original function | A food-related call or root derived from the mouth-opening movement of eating |
| Early meaning | Food as an undivided concept: edible thing, thing to put into the mouth, thing that fills the belly, prey, fruit, root, meat, or food worth carrying back |
| Weak final | The final /m/ may weaken toward a nasal trace or disappear, but the long /aː/ remains central |
| Later derivational potential | food, edible thing, eating, meal, prey, fruit, meat, stored food, nourishment |

`am` is the oldest food-related root currently proposed.

Although the project transcription is `am`, the likely actual pronunciation is not a clipped アム. Because the root is motivated by the mouth-opening movement of eating, the vowel is the core of the word. In calls and ordinary early use, the sound is closer to アァ（ム） or アーム.

The final /m/ represents the closing of the mouth or the act of taking something into the mouth. It may weaken toward a nasalized trace, approaching ン-like resonance or disappearing in some uses. However, the long open vowel /aː/ is expected to remain stable into later stages.

The root originally means "food as a thing" in a broad, undivided sense. It does not yet distinguish prey, fruit, root, meat, stored food, cooked food, or edible but uncertain food.

`am` does not naturally align with the newer restricted `-ia` rule as strongly as `wau` does. Once `-ia` becomes terrain-biased, `am-ia` should no longer be treated as a standard productive form for ordinary food discovery. It may survive only in special older formations if later history requires it.

### `iu`

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Phonetic transcription | `iu`; sign/call form `iiu` |
| Japanese reading | イーウ / イィゥ |
| IPA | /iːu/; weak or silent sign form may preserve mainly the mouth shape |
| Period | Oldest layer, proto-main-peninsula language |
| Original function | A hunting sign or low-voice root used to tell companions that prey had been noticed |
| Early meaning | Prey as an undivided concept: moving food, huntable animal, target that must be noticed quietly before it flees |
| Mouth-shape feature | The long /iː/ mouth shape is held clearly, then closed with /u/ or an almost /m/-like closing gesture |
| Later derivational potential | prey, game animal, hunt, stalking target, animal to be taken, quarry, food-gathering, tracking, hunting action, hunting tool, hunting group |

`iu` is the oldest prey-related root currently proposed.

Unlike `wau` and `am`, this root is not primarily a loud discovery call. It arises from hunting situations where sound could drive the prey away. The important feature is the visible mouth movement: a long, distinctive イ shape is held, then the mouth closes toward ウ or a near ム-like gesture.

This makes the sign useful even with little or no voice. A hunter can signal the presence of prey to nearby companions without shouting.

The early meaning is not yet a strict biological category. It means "the moving food", "the thing to hunt", "the target we must notice quietly", or "the animal that may become food."

`iu` should be treated as a core source for later hunting and food-procurement vocabulary. Many words related to prey, stalking, pursuit, taking animals, hunting parties, hunting tools, and food acquisition can plausibly be built as `iu-` derivatives.

In this family:

- `iu` = prey as a concept / huntable moving food.
- `iu-...` derivatives = words in the hunting, tracking, taking, and food-procurement semantic field.

This derivational pattern is not unique to `iu`. Using an old root as the base for a family of related words is a basic derivational strategy that should be adopted widely for other early roots as well.

### `ra`

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Phonetic transcription | `ra` |
| Japanese reading | ラ / ラァ |
| IPA | /ra/ |
| Period | Oldest layer, proto-main-peninsula language |
| Original function | A loud affirmative report that the expected target exists or has been found |
| Early meaning | It is there, found, expected result obtained, worth coming to |
| Opposite | `en` |
| Later derivational potential | existence, fact, discovery, affirmation, approval, permission, establishment |

`ra` arose as a report word during scouting, movement, and search.

It does not begin as an abstract word for "existence" or "truth". Its oldest use is closer to "the thing we were looking for is there" or "the expected result is present."

Example:

- `ra wau` = there is water; water found.

In shouted use, the vowel may be lengthened: `raa!`.

After the elf-derived `-ia` distinction enters plainfolk speech, `ra` loses some of its necessity as a simple positive search-report marker.

The reason is that a form such as `wau-ia` can already identify the expected type of terrain. A speaker no longer needs `ra` merely to say that the relevant water-feature has been found. As that use weakens, `ra` shifts toward acknowledgement, acceptance, agreement, affirmation, approval, and favorable result.

This makes `ra` increasingly useful as a word for "understood", "accepted", "yes", "that is right", "agreed", or "the desired result has been obtained."

The distinction is pragmatic:

- active report: `wau-ia` = a water-feature is here / the relevant terrain is present.
- passive answer: `ra` = yes / the asked content is affirmed.

### `en`

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Phonetic transcription | `en` |
| Japanese reading | エン / エーン |
| IPA | /en/ |
| Period | Oldest layer, proto-main-peninsula language |
| Original function | A loud negative report that the expected target was not found |
| Early meaning | Not there, not found, not the expected thing, no useful result |
| Opposite | `ra` |
| Later derivational potential | absence, lack, failure, negation, denial, cancellation, grammatical negative |

`en` arose as a negative response to an expected search result.

It is not originally a general logical negator. Its earliest function is practical: someone searched for water, food, safety, or another expected target, then reported that the result was not found.

Example:

- `en wau` = no water; water was not found; this is not water.

In shouted use, the vowel may be lengthened: `een!`.

Over time, this root develops in two related but distinct directions.

As a response word, `en` remains clearly pronounced as エン or エーン. It becomes the direct negative answer: "no", "that is not so", "I reject that", or "wrong".

As a grammatical negative attached to another word, the same root weakens. The final /n/ is not fully pronounced as a clear syllabic ending; it becomes a nasalized trace, almost as if caught in the back of the nose. In project notation, this prefixal form may be recorded as `e(n)-` when needed. It means "non-", "un-", "in-", or "not X", but it must be understood as a phonetic weakening of `en`, not as a spelling-based transformation.

This distinction is important. The negative-response word and the negative prefix share the same origin, but later speakers do not experience them as identical in force:

- `en` = a clear negative answer or rejection.
- `e(n)-X` = X-less, non-X, not X, lacking the quality of X.

Do not derive the negative prefix from written reversal or visual similarity such as `en` -> `ne`. Writing does not exist at this stage, so derivation must follow sound, usage, weakening, and grammaticalization.

### `ma`

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Phonetic transcription | `ma` |
| Japanese reading | マ / マァ |
| IPA | /ma/ |
| Period | Oldest layer, proto-main-peninsula language |
| Original function | A report of uncertainty or suspended judgment |
| Early meaning | Cannot say, unclear, something is there but not the expected thing, needs direct inspection |
| Related terms | `ra`, `en` |
| Later derivational potential | maybe, unknown, unconfirmed, sign, possibility, guess, uncertainty, unnamed thing |

`ma` is the third member of the early exploration-report set.

It covers cases where the speaker cannot truthfully answer `ra` or `en`.

The expected result may not be present, but something relevant may be present. The speaker may lack the vocabulary, knowledge, visibility, or confidence to report clearly. In this case, `ma` signals that companions should come and see.

Important usage distinction:

- `ma wau` = it may be water; something water-related may be there; it is unclear whether this counts as water.
- `ma` = I cannot answer clearly; I do not have the word or judgment for this; come see for yourself.

This root is expected to become highly productive.

Possible later meanings include "maybe", "I do not know", "unconfirmed", "unclear", "it seems", "I think", "sign", "possibility", and "something unnamed."

## Early Three-Answer System

The oldest exploration-report system currently consists of three judgment words plus an object root:

| Form | Early function |
|---|---|
| `ra` | expected target exists / found |
| `en` | expected target does not exist / not found |
| `ma` | unclear / cannot answer / something may be there |
| `wau` | water as a broad undivided concept |

This produces a simple but powerful pattern:

- `ra wau` = water found / there is water.
- `en wau` = no water / not water.
- `ma wau` = maybe water; something water-related; unclear.
- `ma` = cannot say; need others to inspect.

This system marks an important step from simple calls to structured speech. The group can now combine a target with a judgment about the search result.

The roots should not be interpreted as fully modern grammar yet. They are still early roots born from concrete life situations, but they create a basis for later existence, negation, uncertainty, affirmation, and evidential expressions.

After stronger contact with elf speech, the terrain-forming ending `-ia` enters this system and partly replaces the need for `ra` in concrete result reports:

- early form: `ra wau` = water found / there is water.
- contact-influenced form: `wau-ia` = water-feature / shore / river-form / geographic form defined by water.

This is an important early example of elf-language influence changing not only vocabulary but also the division of labor between words. `wau-ia` takes over much of the terrain-report function, while `ra` shifts toward response, acknowledgement, agreement, acceptance, and positive evaluation.

However, `ra` does not disappear from existence-related speech. It remains the natural answer to a question such as "is there water?" In that environment, it means that the questioner's proposition is affirmed.

The same pattern does not apply equally to every noun. Once `-ia` is restricted toward terrain meanings, forms such as `am-ia` or `iu-ia` become less general and require separate historical justification if retained.

Because this contact pattern is productive, the main-peninsula language tends to develop many older terrain words ending in `-ia`. This does not mean every noun must take `-ia`, and it does not mean every `-ia` form is a simple noun plus suffix in a mechanical sense. Rather, the elf-derived ending becomes a common, widely understood option for presenting a terrain feature, topographic formation, or old geographic locus associated with the root.

As `-ia` forms spread, the major early words develop recognizable connected pronunciations:

| Form | Early connected reading | Later or reduced reading |
|---|---|---|
| `wau-ia` | ワゥイァ | ウィア |
| `am-ia` | アァムィァ | アァミァ |
| `iu-ia` | イィウイァ | イィウィァ |

These readings should be treated as pronunciation developments after the spread of `-ia`, not as original spellings. They show how older `-ia` forms begin to behave as familiar words in the local language.

The forms are currently written with `-ia` for analysis. In later orthographies, highly lexicalized one-word forms often no longer preserve a visible `-ia` spelling. For example, a form historically analyzed as `wau-ia` may later be spelled according to its reduced pronunciation rather than as root plus `-ia`. The more fully a form becomes a single word, the more likely its later spelling is to differ from the analytic `-ia` notation.
