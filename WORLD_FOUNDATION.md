\# WORLD FOUNDATION



\## Project Overview



This project is not conventional fantasy worldbuilding.



The purpose is not to create an aesthetically interesting fictional world first and then add culture, history, or civilizations afterward.



Instead, the goal is to construct a world where geography, civilization, culture, language, and history emerge naturally from geological and physical conditions, in a manner broadly consistent with real-world scientific principles.



The world should feel like a real planet with a plausible geological and historical past.



Scientific realism is preferred whenever possible, although perfect scientific accuracy is not required.





\--------------------------------------------------

\## Fundamental Design Philosophy

\--------------------------------------------------



This world has been constructed using the following order of reasoning.



Planetary geology

- Continental formation

- Plate tectonics

- Mountain formation

- Elevation and terrain

- Climate and ocean distribution

- Human migration possibilities

- Civilization isolation or interaction

- Trade routes

- Political structures

- Historical events



This order is extremely important.



History and civilizations were not created first.



The physical world was designed first, and historical developments emerge as a consequence of geography.





\--------------------------------------------------

\## Global Geography Design

\--------------------------------------------------



The world map has already been created through a process based on Earth-like geological assumptions.



The continental layout was not randomly generated.



The continents were designed by studying Earth's historical plate tectonics over hundreds of millions of years and adapting real-world continental drift patterns into a fictional world.



Major continental positions, ocean distribution, and plate movement directions were designed while considering geological plausibility.



The intention is not to perfectly simulate Earth geology, but to avoid obviously unrealistic continental structures.



The world should broadly obey Earth-like tectonic principles.





\--------------------------------------------------

\## Human Migration History

\--------------------------------------------------



At a very ancient stage of history, northern and southern continents were connected by shallow seas, island chains, or temporary land connections.



Early humans were able to migrate between continents during this period.



Over tens of thousands of years, gradual tectonic separation combined with sea level changes eventually created permanent ocean barriers.



By approximately fifty thousand years ago, large-scale migration between northern and southern continents had become impossible.



As a result:



\- populations on each continent became isolated

\- languages developed independently

\- civilizations developed independently

\- cultural exchange disappeared for long periods



Later accidental contact between continents may have occasionally occurred, but systematic migration or regular contact was impossible.





\--------------------------------------------------

\## General Historical Philosophy

\--------------------------------------------------



History should emerge from geography.



Civilizations should not be created arbitrarily.



Political systems should not appear simply because a story requires them.



If a civilization becomes powerful, there should be geographical or economic reasons.



If a culture becomes isolated, there should be geographical reasons.



If languages diverge, migration history should explain why.



Geography creates history.





\--------------------------------------------------

\## Primary Setting Region

\--------------------------------------------------



The main story takes place on a peninsula region.



This peninsula is divided into three major regions.



South Region

West Region

Northeast Region





South Region:



\- contains large fertile plains

\- contains the primary capital city

\- possesses favorable port access

\- serves as the center of long-distance southern maritime trade





West Region:



\- culturally close to South Region

\- connected to northern continental coastal trade

\- has more direct contact with foreign states

\- historically became more sensitive to external geopolitical threats





Northeast Region:



\- geographically more isolated

\- less fertile land

\- developed somewhat independently from South and West regions

\- culturally related but historically less integrated





South and West gradually developed into a shared cultural sphere.



Northeast remained relatively separate.





\--------------------------------------------------

\## Major Volcanic Event

\--------------------------------------------------



Approximately one hundred years before the present setting, a volcanic eruption occurred near the northeastern offshore region.



This eruption was not apocalyptic.



It did not destroy civilization directly.



The main consequences were:



\- widespread ashfall across the peninsula

\- severe agricultural damage

\- particularly severe food shortages in Northeast Region

\- migration of refugees toward South and West



The eruption weakened the peninsula as a whole and accelerated political change.





\--------------------------------------------------

\## Development of Maritime Technology

\--------------------------------------------------



Around the same historical period, shipbuilding and navigation technology began improving significantly.



For the first time in history:



\- southern continent trade became realistically possible

\- trade with northwestern continental powers increased

\- long-distance maritime routes began developing



However, maritime technology remained limited.



Open ocean navigation was still dangerous.



Most trade remained coastal or relatively short-range.





\--------------------------------------------------

\## Political Transformation of the Peninsula

\--------------------------------------------------



Before this period, the peninsula remained politically fragmented.



South Region had already favored political unification long before recent events.



The volcanic disaster was not the primary cause of political unification.



The primary cause was geopolitical pressure.



West Region had frequent contact with foreign civilizations through coastal trade.



As a result, West Region became increasingly aware of external powers and began recognizing the danger of remaining politically fragmented.



West Region gradually shifted toward supporting political unification.



Because South Region had already favored political integration, both regions aligned politically.



Over roughly forty to fifty years, this process resulted in imperial unification.





\--------------------------------------------------

\## Important Perspective For Future Work

\--------------------------------------------------



When developing future worldbuilding, avoid creating history in isolation.



Always consider the causal chain.



Questions should always begin from:



Why did geography make this possible?



Why did people settle here?



Why did trade develop here?



Why did political power concentrate here?



Why did civilizations diverge?



Why did historical events happen under these conditions?



The objective is not fantasy aesthetics.



The objective is constructing a believable world whose history emerges naturally from geography.



\--------------------------------------------------

\## Plains Settlement Pattern and Urban Density

\--------------------------------------------------



In the main peninsula plains, settlement density should generally follow water systems and roads.



Major rivers, lakes, irrigation channels, bridges, and passable roads create settlement corridors.



The pattern should not be perfectly radial or evenly spaced.



Instead:



\- areas near a major city are denser

\- agricultural land spreads continuously near the urban core

\- smaller villages and towns appear farther outward

\- settlement spacing widens where water access or road access is weaker

\- road and river junctions naturally become secondary nodes



For a major productive city in a large plain, smaller towns and villages appearing roughly ten to twenty kilometers apart can be plausible, but only where water, roads, and terrain support it.



\--------------------------------------------------

\## Visual Reference Use

\--------------------------------------------------



Generated images may be used as working reference material for atmosphere, scale, road density, agricultural spread, and temporal comparison between historical periods.



They should not automatically be treated as exact specification in every detail.



Particular caution is required for:



\- wagon construction

\- clothing and status markers

\- architectural detail

\- road engineering detail

\- fine infrastructure detail



Visual references are useful for confirming whether the world specification produces the intended image.



If a generated image reveals something unclear or inconsistent, the world specification may be refined afterward.

\## Combat Equipment and Battlefield Function

\--------------------------------------------------



Weapon value should not be ranked only by abstract strength.



Its value depends on:



\- who uses it

\- for what role

\- in what terrain

\- with what support



Common shield-bearing swordsmen are expected to favor realistic one-handed weapons, especially short sword to broadsword range, often together with a buckler or a reduced kite-shaped shield.



Longswords are not rare, but they are less favored in daily escort, scouting, and exploratory mercenary work because they are less convenient to carry and use in confined, irregular, or mobile situations.



However, in military use or anti-monster operations, longswords can be highly effective when deployed under command and formation support.



Rear-line fighters are not simply weak combatants behind the front.



They often carry heavy responsibility for:



\- protecting wounded personnel

\- recovering casualties

\- protecting casters

\- deciding when to withdraw, reposition, or stabilize the rear



Because of this, rear-line personnel with strong judgment and command ability are rare and highly valued.



Casters should not be treated as fantasy artillery by default.



In many combat situations, a mage or priest uses a limited number of support actions, then prioritizes concealment and survival rather than continuous attack casting.



General battlefield treatment should be handled first by physical first aid.



Magical intervention is best treated as an advanced life-saving tool:



\- bleeding control

\- wound closure

\- fracture stabilization

\- pain suppression

\- emergency rescue of otherwise fatal injuries
