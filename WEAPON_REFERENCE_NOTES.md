# Weapon Reference Notes

## Document Role

This document stores reference notes for thinking about weapon design, weapon categories, and their historical plausibility inside the world specification.

It is not a hard rule file.

It is not a direct image-generation priority sheet.

Instead, it is a design-reference memo for answering questions such as:

- what kinds of weapons may plausibly exist
- why they would appear early or late
- what jobs or fighting styles would favor them
- how world-specific conditions such as magic, monsters, political stability, and social structure affect weapon choice

Use this document as background design material when creating visual references, equipment samples, or later formal specifications.

## Core Method

Weapon history in this project should not be copied mechanically from real history.

The correct question is not:

- "did this exact weapon exist in that exact century in the real world?"

The correct questions are:

- what technological level exists here
- what fighting style exists here
- what social need exists here
- what materials and manufacturing precision exist here
- what threats exist here

If a weapon type becomes necessary earlier in this world, and the civilization level could plausibly produce it, then an earlier analogue is acceptable even if real-world history placed it later.

Likewise, if a real-world weapon existed by a certain century but this world lacks the need or cultural basis for it, then it should not automatically appear as common equipment here.

## Civilization Baseline

For weapons, equipment, clothing, and adornment, the project often uses a real-civilization baseline roughly comparable to the 12th to 13th centuries, or in some ancient-state contexts the Roman imperial peak, as a starting point for thinking.

This baseline is not a hard museum-catalog restriction.

It is a way to estimate:

- metallurgy
- manufacturing precision
- carrying culture
- training burden
- maintenance burden
- daily practicality

After establishing that baseline, world-specific reasons may justify divergence.

## Mage-State Elf Equipment Context

For the elf mage-state around 1000 to 800 years ago:

- the state is politically stable and broadly unified
- large-scale war and chronic invasion are not the main everyday pressure
- magic is the dominant source of combat superiority
- weapons are often for self-defense, internal security, controlled force, deterrence, ceremonial authority, and specialized operations rather than constant battlefield survival

Because of this, elf equipment should not be assumed to optimize for heavy war culture.

General tendencies may include:

- practical and refined sidearms
- slender light throwing spears or javelin-like weapons as plausible common secondary or general-purpose arms
- weapons that do not interfere heavily with magic use
- lower emphasis on very large always-carried weapons
- greater emphasis on control, dignity, and disciplined readiness

This does not mean that long weapons or strong melee weapons never exist.

It means they should not automatically become the everyday default of elf society.

For mage-state elf society specifically, a slender javelin-like weapon may plausibly be common because it suits:

- internal security and controlled force
- deterrence and readiness without heavy battlefield burden
- short-range throwing and light suppression
- users who are not optimized for heavy sustained melee

## Plainfolk and Later Inheritance

Main peninsula plainfolk culture is strongly influenced by earlier elf mage-state culture.

Therefore:

- some carrying methods
- some silhouettes
- some decorative logic
- some material preferences
- some ideas of what counts as "civilized" equipment

may descend from elf precedent, even if plainfolk later simplify, adapt, or reinterpret them for their own bodies, trades, and fighting styles.

## Job and Fighting Style Over Weapon Taxonomy

Weapon choice should usually be derived from:

- job type
- fighting style
- expected threat
- need for travel
- solo versus team operation
- civilian, military, police, or specialized context

Avoid treating one weapon form as universally correct merely because it appears efficient in one prompt.

## Compact Weapons in Escort and Exploration Work

For escort, exploration, courier, and similar road-oriented work, relatively compact weapons often become natural because they are easier to carry, draw, control, and use in roads, buildings, camps, inns, carts, and uneven ground.

Common examples that may often appear in such contexts include:

- smaller one-handed swords
- short swords
- maces
- hand axes
- short spears

This is a tendency, not a universal rule.

Exceptions may arise from:

- local culture
- monster threat profile
- personal training
- team tactics
- class or official duty

## Long Swords and Large Weapons

Long swords or larger melee weapons should not be treated as forbidden.

However, they may be less common as everyday equipment in solo or travel-heavy professions.

They become more plausible in contexts such as:

- military deployment
- anti-monster operations
- formation support
- specialized heavy hitters
- trained team tactics

## Monster Pressure and Early Weapon Divergence

Because this world contains monsters, weapon development may diverge from real history.

If local threats include creatures resembling large bulls, horses, bears, or similarly robust bodies, then cutting weapons may not always be preferred.

This creates plausible early pressure toward:

- clubs
- maces
- hammers
- reinforced impact weapons
- specialized anti-monster melee tools

## Morning-Star-Type Weapons and Impact Weapons

Even if a morning-star-like weapon was not standard in the analogous real-world century, a comparable weapon may appear earlier here if:

- monster suppression creates demand
- impact damage is valued over cutting
- the relevant manufacturing skill exists

Still, not all impact weapons should be treated equally.

Simpler impact weapons such as maces and clubs are easier to generalize.

Chain-based or more specialized striking weapons are more likely to belong to:

- trained personnel
- specialized anti-monster teams
- local security traditions
- specific regional doctrines

## Government, Security, and Local Defense

Monster response is often better treated as an organized operation by government, military, or policing forces rather than as random individual heroism.

This makes it plausible that:

- some units standardize impact weapons for specific roles
- those personnel also carry sidearms such as short swords or daggers
- village self-defense groups may adopt similar tools where state presence is weak
- even casters may carry compact impact or sidearm weapons for emergency self-defense
